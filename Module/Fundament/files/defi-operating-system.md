# PART I — LEARNING CURRICULUM

## Stage 1: Intermediate Foundation (Weeks 1–4)

### 1.1 — Money Markets: Beyond the Basics

You understand supply, borrow, collateral, LTV, health factor, and liquidation. Now go deeper.

**Utilization Rate Dynamics.** Every lending pool has a utilization curve — the relationship between how much of the pool is borrowed and the interest rate charged. Most protocols (Aave, Compound) use a kinked rate model: rates increase slowly until a "kink point" (typically 80–90% utilization), then spike aggressively. This kink exists to always preserve exit liquidity for depositors. When utilization pushes past the kink, borrowers get punished with extreme rates, incentivizing repayment. Understanding where the kink sits and how fast rates accelerate above it tells you whether a lending position will remain profitable or bleed out overnight.

**Reserve Factor.** Not all interest paid by borrowers goes to suppliers. Protocols take a cut — the reserve factor — typically 10–30%. This funds the protocol treasury and acts as a buffer against bad debt. When you see "supply APY" on Aave, you're seeing the post-reserve number. The actual gross yield generated by borrowers is higher. This matters when comparing protocols: a protocol with a lower reserve factor passes more yield to you.

**Isolation Mode & E-Mode.** Aave V3 introduced these. Isolation mode lets governance list new, riskier assets without exposing the entire protocol — isolated assets can only borrow stablecoins up to a debt ceiling. E-Mode (efficiency mode) groups correlated assets (e.g., ETH/wstETH/cbETH) and allows higher LTV between them — sometimes 93%+ instead of 80%. E-Mode is the foundation of leverage loops (covered in Stage 2).

**Bad Debt Mechanics.** When a position is liquidated but the collateral doesn't fully cover the debt (because price moved too fast, or liquidators didn't act in time), the protocol absorbs "bad debt." This bad debt is socialized — it comes from the reserve pool, or in extreme cases, from a shortfall event that hits token stakers (like Aave's Safety Module). Understanding bad debt tells you the real tail risk of supplying assets to a protocol.

### 1.2 — AMMs & Liquidity Provision

**Constant Product Formula.** Uniswap V2 and its forks use x × y = k. This means the pool always maintains a mathematical relationship between two assets. When someone buys token A, they push token B into the pool, and the price adjusts along the curve. You as LP provide both sides of the pool and earn fees — but your position rebalances against you continuously. This is impermanent loss.

**Impermanent Loss — The Real Math.** IL is not a bug; it's the cost of providing liquidity. If one asset moves 2x relative to the other, you lose ~5.7% compared to holding. At 3x divergence, ~13.4%. At 5x, ~25.5%. The formula: IL = 2√(price_ratio) / (1 + price_ratio) − 1. This is permanent once you withdraw. It's only "impermanent" if prices revert before you exit. Most retail LPs lose money net of IL because they underestimate divergence risk and overestimate fee APY.

**Concentrated Liquidity (Uniswap V3 / V4).** Instead of spreading liquidity across the full 0-to-infinity price range, V3 lets you choose a range. Narrower range = more capital efficiency = more fees per dollar deployed — but also more IL if price leaves your range. When price exits your range, you hold 100% of the losing asset and earn zero fees. This is a fundamentally different product than V2: you are actively market-making with directional exposure.

**Key Metric: Fee APY vs. IL.** The only question that matters for any LP position: do the fees earned exceed the impermanent loss? Most analytics dashboards show APY based on fees alone. You need to calculate net PnL = fees earned − IL − gas costs. Tools like Revert Finance show this for Uniswap V3 positions.

### 1.3 — Stablecoins: The Risk Spectrum

Not all stablecoins are equal. They sit on a risk spectrum:

**Fiat-Backed (USDC, USDT).** Centralized, backed by real-world reserves. Risk: custodian risk, regulatory seizure, bank failure (USDC depegged to $0.87 during SVB collapse in March 2023). USDC has blocklist capability — Circle can freeze your tokens.

**Over-Collateralized (DAI/USDS, LUSD, crvUSD).** Backed by crypto collateral locked in smart contracts. DAI is mostly backed by USDC and RWAs now — it's a wrapper around centralized assets. LUSD (Liquity V1) is backed purely by ETH. crvUSD uses a novel "soft liquidation" mechanism (LLAMMA). Risk: oracle failure, governance attack, black swan collateral crash.

**Algorithmic / Endogenous (historical: UST).** Backed by nothing except mechanism design and reflexive token economics. UST/LUNA proved that reflexive backing creates death spirals. Any stablecoin that maintains its peg through its own token's market cap is fundamentally fragile.

**Delta-Neutral Synthetic (USDe by Ethena).** Backed by a hedged position: hold stETH, short ETH perpetuals. The short pays funding rate (usually positive = yield). Risk: negative funding periods, exchange counterparty risk, stETH depeg, liquidity crisis during unwind. USDe is not a stablecoin in the traditional sense — it's a structured product.

Understanding the risk spectrum of stablecoins is prerequisite knowledge for every DeFi strategy.

## Stage 2: Advanced Mechanics (Weeks 5–10)

### 2.1 — Leverage Loops

A leverage loop amplifies yield by recursively supplying and borrowing the same or correlated assets.

**ETH Staking Leverage Loop (Classic Example):**
1. Deposit 10 ETH worth of wstETH into Aave V3 (E-Mode).
2. Borrow 9 ETH against it (90% LTV in E-Mode).
3. Swap borrowed ETH → wstETH.
4. Deposit the new wstETH.
5. Borrow again.
6. Repeat 3–5 times.

At 3x effective leverage, you earn ~3x the stETH yield (~3.5% × 3 = ~10.5%) minus the ETH borrow cost (~2–4%). Net yield: ~6–8% on your original capital, denominated in ETH.

**When It Works:** When staking yield exceeds borrow cost, and the stETH/ETH peg holds. This has been consistently profitable since the Merge because the spread is structural (staking yield exists, ETH borrow demand is moderate).

**When It Breaks:** If stETH depegs (like June 2022, when it hit 0.93 ETH), your collateral loses value faster than your debt, triggering liquidation. At 3x leverage, a 5–7% depeg can liquidate you. Also breaks if ETH borrow rates spike above staking yield (happens during high-demand periods).

**Risk Management:** Keep leverage under 2.5x. Monitor stETH/ETH ratio daily. Set health factor alerts. Have a pre-planned deleverage trigger (e.g., if stETH/ETH < 0.98, begin unwinding).

### 2.2 — Liquidation Cascades

Liquidation cascades are the systemic risk event of DeFi. Here's the mechanics:

1. Price drops → positions approach liquidation threshold.
2. Liquidators buy collateral at discount (typically 5–15% bonus).
3. Liquidated collateral gets sold on DEXs → more sell pressure.
4. More sell pressure → price drops further → more liquidations triggered.
5. If DEX liquidity is thin, liquidation sales cause massive slippage → price crashes harder.
6. Protocol accrues bad debt if liquidations can't clear profitably.

This is a reflexive feedback loop. It's why DeFi crashes are faster and more violent than TradFi crashes. The cascades compound on-chain in real time.

**Where to Monitor:** DeFiLlama liquidation dashboards, Parsec Finance, and EigenPhi show real-time liquidation walls — price levels where large positions get liquidated. These levels act as gravity wells during drawdowns.

### 2.3 — Capital Efficiency

Capital efficiency = how much economic output you generate per dollar locked.

**Uniswap V2:** 100% of your capital is deployed, but most sits in unused price ranges. Extremely capital inefficient — you might use 1–5% of your liquidity for actual trades.

**Uniswap V3 Concentrated LP:** If you concentrate in a ±5% range, you get ~10–20x the capital efficiency of V2. But you're taking on active management risk.

**Aave E-Mode Loops:** By borrowing at 93% LTV and redepositing, you turn 1 ETH of capital into 3–5 ETH of economic exposure. High capital efficiency, but fragile.

**Morpho:** Peer-to-peer matching layer on top of Aave/Compound. Matches lenders directly with borrowers at better rates for both, improving capital efficiency of the underlying pool.

**Pendle:** Splits yield-bearing assets into principal tokens (PT) and yield tokens (YT). You can buy fixed yield (PT at discount) or leveraged yield (YT). This is rate trading — the DeFi equivalent of interest rate derivatives. Extremely capital efficient for yield plays.

### 2.4 — Delta-Neutral Strategies

Delta-neutral means your position has zero directional exposure to the underlying asset's price. You profit from yield, funding, or volatility — not from price movement.

**Funding Rate Arbitrage:**
1. Hold spot ETH (or stETH).
2. Open a short ETH perpetual for the same notional value.
3. Net price exposure: zero.
4. Profit: staking yield + funding rate (when positive) − borrowing costs.

In bull markets, perpetual funding rates are positive (longs pay shorts), so the short leg earns additional yield. This is Ethena's USDe mechanism at its core.

**Risk:** Negative funding in bear markets erodes returns. Exchange counterparty risk (FTX taught this lesson). Basis risk between spot and perp prices. Liquidity risk during extreme volatility (you might not be able to close both legs simultaneously).

**Lending Carry Trade:**
1. Borrow stablecoins at 3% on Aave.
2. Deploy into a higher-yield protocol at 8% (e.g., Pendle PT, or a vault strategy).
3. Pocket the 5% spread.

Risk: the higher-yield source carries smart contract risk, liquidity risk, or could be a temporarily inflated incentive that drops. Variable borrow rates can spike and eliminate the spread.

### 2.5 — Points, Airdrops & Incentive Farming

Most protocols launch with point systems that convert to token airdrops. This is effectively a subsidy — the protocol pays you (in future tokens) to use it early.

**Economics:** Airdrop farming is rational when the expected value of the airdrop exceeds your opportunity cost and risk. The problem: expected value is speculative. You're pricing an unknown token at an unknown FDV at an unknown date.

**Strategy Framework:**
- Prioritize protocols with strong backers (Paradigm, a16z, etc.) — higher likelihood of a valuable token.
- Calculate your capital cost. If you lock $10k for 6 months, your opportunity cost is ~$300–500 (5% risk-free rate).
- Diversify across 3–5 protocols rather than concentrating.
- Monitor Sybil detection — protocols increasingly filter out multi-wallet farmers.
- Never use borrowed money for airdrop farming. The timing is uncertain, and you pay interest on a speculative outcome.

## Stage 3: Professional Level (Weeks 11–16)

### 3.1 — Protocol Risk Analysis

Professional DeFi operators evaluate every protocol across these dimensions:

**Smart Contract Risk:**
- Is the code audited? By whom? (Trail of Bits, OpenZeppelin, Spearbit are top-tier.)
- Is it a fork? Of what? Has the original been battle-tested?
- How long has the code been live with significant TVL? Time is the best audit.
- Is there an active bug bounty? How large? (Immunefi listings.)
- Are there timelocks on admin functions? Multisig? What's the signer composition?

**Oracle Risk:**
- Which oracle does the protocol use? (Chainlink, Pyth, UMA, custom TWAP.)
- What happens if the oracle delivers a stale or manipulated price?
- Flash loan oracle attacks: can someone manipulate the price source within a single transaction?
- Chainlink's decentralization varies per feed — some feeds have only 3–4 node operators.

**Governance Risk:**
- Who controls parameter changes? (Multisig, DAO vote, timelock.)
- Can governance drain user funds? (Some protocols have "guardian" keys that can pause or upgrade contracts.)
- What's the token distribution? If insiders hold 40%+ of voting power, governance is effectively centralized.

**Liquidity Risk:**
- Can you exit your position at the size you entered? At what slippage?
- For lending: what happens if utilization hits 100%? (You can't withdraw.)
- For LP positions: what's the pool depth relative to your position size?

**Dependency Risk:**
- Does the protocol depend on other protocols? (Composability = dependency chains.)
- If Chainlink goes down, what breaks? If Aave gets exploited, what protocols built on top of it also fail?
- Bridge dependencies: any asset bridged from another chain carries the bridge's security assumptions.

### 3.2 — Systemic Risk in DeFi

DeFi's composability means protocols are interconnected. Systemic risk arises when a failure in one protocol cascades through the stack.

**Layers of Systemic Risk:**

Layer 1 — Base chain (Ethereum, Arbitrum). If the chain halts or is censored, everything on it stops.

Layer 2 — Core primitives (Aave, Uniswap, Maker/Sky). These are systemically important. An Aave exploit would cascade into every protocol that uses aTokens as collateral.

Layer 3 — Middleware (oracles, bridges, keepers). Often invisible but critical. The Wormhole bridge exploit ($320M) and Ronin bridge hack ($625M) demonstrated this.

Layer 4 — Application layer (yield aggregators, structured products). These stack risk from every layer below.

**Correlation Risk:** In a DeFi crash, all assets become correlated. ETH drops, stETH depegs, stablecoin pools become unbalanced, liquidation cascades trigger across Aave/Compound simultaneously, DEX liquidity evaporates. Your "diversified" portfolio across 5 protocols is actually one trade: long DeFi infrastructure.

### 3.3 — Treasury & DAO Strategies

DAOs manage treasuries using many of the same strategies, at scale:

**Diversification into Stables:** Most DAOs hold 20–40% of their treasury in stablecoins — operational runway. Typically split between USDC (liquidity) and DAI/sDAI (yield).

**Protocol-Owned Liquidity (POL):** Instead of renting liquidity through incentive emissions, DAOs own their own LP positions. Olympus DAO pioneered this (bond mechanism). Pros: sustainable, no mercenary capital. Cons: IL exposure, capital locked.

**Yield on Idle Assets:** DAOs deploy idle treasury into low-risk yield — sDAI, Aave supply, short-duration Pendle PTs. The key constraint: DAOs need to exit without moving markets, so they can only use the most liquid venues.

**Strategic Token Holdings:** Some DAOs hold governance tokens of protocols they depend on, creating alignment. Example: a lending protocol holding CRV to direct Curve gauge incentives toward their pools.

### 3.4 — Designing Your Own Strategies

At the professional level, you stop copying strategies and start designing them. The framework:

1. **Identify the yield source.** Where does the money come from? Borrow demand, trading fees, funding rates, protocol incentives, or arbitrage? If you can't name the source, you don't understand the strategy.

2. **Map the risks.** What can break? List every dependency: smart contracts, oracles, pegs, liquidity, governance, counterparties.

3. **Size the position.** Professional DeFi operators never put more than 5–15% of capital in a single strategy. Most run 5–10 strategies simultaneously with varying risk profiles.

4. **Define exit conditions.** Before entering: what triggers an exit? Health factor below X, APY drops below Y, TVL drops by Z%, audit flag. Write these down.

5. **Backtest with history.** Would this strategy have survived March 2020? May 2021 crash? Luna collapse (May 2022)? FTX (November 2022)? SVB/USDC depeg (March 2023)? If not, understand what would have happened to your capital.

6. **Monitor and iterate.** DeFi is not set-and-forget. Strategies decay as they get crowded. Yields compress. New risks emerge. A strategy that worked in January might be unprofitable by March.

# PART II — RISK MANAGEMENT FRAMEWORK

## The Risk Stack

Every DeFi position carries multiple overlapping risks. Think of them as layers:

### Layer 1: Smart Contract Risk
The code might have bugs. Even audited code. Even code that's been live for years (see Euler Finance exploit — $197M, March 2023, after 2 years of operation).

**Mitigation:** Diversify across protocols. Never put >20% of capital in any single smart contract system. Prioritize battle-tested code (Aave, Uniswap, Maker have survived multiple stress tests). Check if the protocol has insurance coverage (Nexus Mutual, InsurAce).

### Layer 2: Oracle Risk
DeFi protocols need external price data. If the oracle is wrong, liquidations fire incorrectly, or positions get exploited.

**Mitigation:** Prefer protocols using Chainlink (industry standard, most decentralized). Be cautious with protocols using custom TWAP oracles (manipulable with capital). Avoid protocols using a single oracle source.

### Layer 3: Liquidity Risk
You can enter a position but can't exit at the expected price — or at all.

**Mitigation:** Never be more than 5% of a pool's TVL. Check utilization rates on lending pools before supplying. For LP positions, ensure the pool has consistent volume (fees only materialize with volume).

### Layer 4: Stablecoin / Peg Risk
Your "stable" asset loses its peg.

**Mitigation:** Diversify stablecoin exposure. Understand the collateral backing of every stablecoin you hold. Avoid algorithmic stablecoins for capital preservation. Keep a portion in USDC for maximum liquidity despite its centralization risks.

### Layer 5: Bridge Risk
Any asset on a non-native chain carries bridge security assumptions.

**Mitigation:** Prefer native assets on each chain. If using bridged assets, prefer canonical bridges (Arbitrum's native bridge, Optimism's native bridge) over third-party bridges. Never store significant capital in bridged tokens longer than necessary.

### Layer 6: Governance Risk
Protocol governance changes parameters that affect your position — LTV ratios, interest rate curves, liquidation bonuses, supported collateral.

**Mitigation:** Follow governance forums of protocols you're deployed in. Set alerts for governance proposals. Understand who controls upgrades (multisig composition, timelock duration).

## Position Sizing Rules

- Maximum 20% of total DeFi capital in any single protocol.
- Maximum 5–10% in any single strategy.
- Keep 20–30% in stablecoins or easily accessible assets (dry powder for opportunities and drawdown management).
- Total DeFi allocation should be a defined percentage of net worth — not everything.

## Health Factor Management

For leveraged lending positions:
- Never let health factor drop below 1.5 in normal conditions.
- Set alerts at health factor 1.8 (warning) and 1.5 (action required).
- Pre-plan your deleveraging steps: which asset do you sell first, on which DEX, what's the gas cost under stress conditions.
- Remember: gas prices spike during market crashes (everyone is transacting simultaneously). Your $5 deleverage transaction might cost $50–200 during peak stress.

# PART III — DeFi OPERATING SYSTEM

## Daily Workflow (15–20 minutes)

**Morning Check:**
- Check portfolio dashboard (DeBank or Zapper) — health factors, position values, accrued yields.
- Scan DeFi Twitter/X for overnight incidents (exploits, depegs, governance drama).
- Check gas prices — if abnormally high, something is happening.

**Midday Scan (optional):**
- Review any positions near alert thresholds.
- Check funding rates if running delta-neutral strategies.

**Evening Review:**
- Log any actions taken and updated position values.
- Check governance proposals in protocols you're deployed in.

## Weekly Workflow (60–90 minutes, one session)

**Research Block (30 min):**
- Deep-read one protocol's documentation or one DeFi research report.
- Evaluate one new opportunity using the protocol evaluation checklist.

**Portfolio Review (20 min):**
- Calculate actual net returns (gross yield − IL − gas − opportunity cost).
- Compare performance vs. simply holding.
- Decide: rebalance, exit, or increase any positions.

**Learning Block (20 min):**
- Follow the curriculum stage you're in.
- Write a short note summarizing what you learned (forces synthesis).

## Protocol Evaluation Checklist

When evaluating any new protocol or opportunity, answer these questions:

1. **Where does the yield come from?** (Borrow demand / trading fees / incentive emissions / funding rates / arbitrage)
2. **Is the yield sustainable or subsidized?** (Token emissions = subsidized. Will compress when incentives end.)
3. **What are the smart contract risk factors?** (Audits, time live, TVL history, past incidents.)
4. **What are the oracle dependencies?** (Chainlink, Pyth, custom, none.)
5. **What's the governance structure?** (Multisig, DAO, timelock, upgradeable.)
6. **What's the liquidity profile?** (Can I exit at my position size without >1% slippage?)
7. **What's the worst-case scenario?** (Total loss? Temporary freeze? Depeg?)
8. **What's my edge?** (Am I early? Do I understand something the market doesn't? Or am I chasing APY like everyone else?)

If you can't answer question 1 clearly, don't enter the position. If you can't answer question 8, you're retail.

# PART VI — MENTAL MODELS FOR DeFi

These aren't in any textbook. They come from experience.

**1. "Where does the yield come from?"**
If you can't answer this in one sentence, you don't understand the position. Every yield in DeFi has a source: someone is paying for something (borrowing, trading, hedging, speculating). If the source is "token emissions," the yield is subsidized and will compress. If the source is "new depositors," it might be a Ponzi.

**2. "What am I the counterparty to?"**
Every position in DeFi makes you a counterparty. As a lender, you're the counterparty to borrowers who might get liquidated into bad debt. As an LP, you're the counterparty to informed traders who extract value from your position. As a yield farmer, you might be the counterparty to a protocol's insiders who will dump on you. Name your counterparty.

**3. "Composability is dependency."**
DeFi's superpower — protocols plugging into each other — is also its systemic risk vector. Every layer of composability adds a dependency. A yield vault that deposits into Aave which uses Chainlink oracles on Ethereum L1 has four layers of dependency. Each can fail independently.

**4. "In a crash, all correlations go to 1."**
Diversifying across DeFi protocols feels safe. But in a systemic event (like Luna, FTX, or a major exploit), all DeFi assets sell off together. The only true diversification is between DeFi and non-DeFi assets. Within DeFi, position sizing and cash reserves are your defense.

**5. "The APY is not your return."**
APY is the gross rate. Your actual return is: APY − IL − gas costs − opportunity cost − time spent managing − risk premium you should have charged. Most retail DeFi users would have been better off holding ETH. Be honest with yourself about net returns.

**6. "Liquidity is permission to be wrong."**
The most important feature of any position is: can you exit? Illiquid positions with high APY are traps. You can't rebalance, you can't cut losses, you can't respond to new information. Always verify exit liquidity before entry.
