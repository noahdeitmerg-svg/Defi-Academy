Wallet Fundamentals

Before you interact with any DeFi protocol, you need a wallet. Your wallet is your identity, your bank account, and your security perimeter in Web3.

What Is a Crypto Wallet?

A crypto wallet is software that stores your private keys — the cryptographic proof that you control your assets. When you "send" crypto, you're signing a transaction with your private key. The wallet itself doesn't hold tokens — they live on the blockchain. The wallet holds the key that proves they're yours.

Security Essentials

More money is lost to poor security practices than to market crashes. This lesson teaches you the habits that protect your capital.

The Three Rules

Rule 1: Never share your seed phrase
No legitimate protocol, support agent, or wallet will ever ask for your seed phrase. If someone asks, it's a scam — 100% of the time, with zero exceptions.
Rule 2: Verify before you sign
Every transaction you sign with your wallet is a binding instruction. Use Rabby's transaction preview to see exactly what will happen. If a "free mint" asks to approve unlimited token spending, reject it immediately.
Rule 3: Manage your approvals
When you approve a DeFi protocol to spend your tokens, that approval persists forever unless you revoke it. Use revoke.cash to regularly audit and revoke unnecessary approvals.

Security Checklist

  • Seed phrase written on paper and stored offline
  • No digital copy of seed phrase anywhere (no photos, no notes apps, no cloud)
  • Rabby Wallet installed with transaction simulation enabled
  • Bookmarked revoke.cash for monthly approval audits
  • Using a separate "DeFi browser" profile (no personal email or social media)
  • Hardware wallet purchased from official retailer (for amounts > $1,000)

Smart Contracts

A smart contract is a program that lives on the blockchain. It executes automatically when conditions are met. Every DeFi protocol is a set of smart contracts.

What Makes Them "Smart"?

Smart contracts are deterministic — given the same inputs, they always produce the same outputs. They're trustless — you don't need to trust the developer because the code is public and auditable. And they're immutable — once deployed, the code cannot be changed (unless the contract was specifically designed to be upgradeable).

The website can go down, but the smart contract stays on the blockchain. If Uniswap.org went offline tomorrow, the Uniswap contracts would still work — you'd just need a different interface to interact with them.

Reading a Contract on Etherscan

Every smart contract is viewable on Etherscan (or Basescan, Arbiscan for L2s). You can see:

  • Read functions — query data without paying gas (e.g., check your balance)
  • Write functions — change state and cost gas (e.g., deposit, withdraw)
  • Events/Logs — a record of everything the contract has done
  • Source code — if verified, you can read the actual Solidity code

Every operation on Ethereum costs gas — it's the fee you pay validators to process your transaction. Understanding gas prevents you from overpaying or getting stuck.

AMM Mechanics

Decentralized exchanges don't use order books. Instead, they use Automated Market Makers — mathematical formulas that always quote a price based on the ratio of tokens in a pool.

The Constant Product Formula

Uniswap V2 (and most AMM forks) uses a deceptively simple formula:

x × y = k Where: x = quantity of Token A in the pool y = quantity of Token B in the pool k = a constant (the product must stay the same after every trade) Example: Pool has 100 ETH and 200,000 USDC k = 100 × 200,000 = 20,000,000 Implied price = 200,000 / 100 = $2,000 per ETH Someone buys 1 ETH: New ETH in pool = 99 New USDC needed: 20,000,000 / 99 = 202,020 USDC Buyer paid: 202,020 - 200,000 = 2,020 USDC for 1 ETH New price = 202,020 / 99 = $2,040.61 per ETH

The price moved because the trade changed the ratio. This is called price impact — the larger your trade relative to the pool, the more the price moves against you.

Impermanent Loss

The price you pay for providing liquidity. Impermanent loss (IL) occurs because the AMM automatically rebalances your position — selling the winning asset and buying the losing one.

The Math

IL = 2 × √(price_ratio) / (1 + price_ratio) − 1 Where price_ratio = new_price / original_price

IL at Different Price Changes

Price ChangeImpermanent LossIn Dollar Terms ($10,000 position)
±25%-0.6%-$60
±50%-2.0%-$200
2x (100% up)-5.7%-$570
3x (200% up)-13.4%-$1,340

Critical Understanding
IL is symmetric — a 2x price increase and a 50% price decrease produce the same IL percentage. But the dollar impact is worse when price drops because your remaining assets are worth less. The loss becomes "permanent" if you withdraw while prices are diverged.

When LP Is Profitable

The only question that matters: do fees earned exceed IL?

  • High correlation pairs (ETH/stETH, USDC/USDT) — IL is minimal because prices move together
  • High volume / low TVL — more trades per dollar of liquidity = more fees per LP
  • Incentivized pools — additional token rewards offset IL

If none of these apply, you'll almost certainly underperform simply holding the tokens.

Utilization Rate

The utilization rate is the percentage of deposited assets currently borrowed. It's the most important metric for understanding a lending market's health:

Utilization = Total Borrowed / Total Supplied Example: 80M USDC borrowed out of 100M supplied = 80% utilization

When utilization is too high, lenders can't withdraw — there's literally no available liquidity in the pool. This is why interest rate curves spike aggressively above optimal utilization.

Interest Rate Curves

DeFi lending rates aren't set by a central bank. They're calculated algorithmically based on supply and demand, expressed through the utilization rate.

The Kink Model

Most lending protocols use a "kinked" interest rate curve — rates increase slowly below optimal utilization, then spike dramatically above it.

Real Example: Aave USDC Market

Parameters: Base rate = 0%, Slope1 = 4%, Optimal = 90%, Slope2 = 60% At 45% utilization: rate = (45/90) × 4% = 2.0% At 80% utilization: rate = (80/90) × 4% = 3.6% At 90% utilization: rate = 4% = 4.0% ← KINK At 95% utilization: rate = 4% + (5/10) × 60% = 34% ← PAIN At 99% utilization: rate = 4% + (9/10) × 60% = 58% ← EXTREME
Strategic Implication
If you're borrowing at 4% to leverage-loop for 7%, a utilization spike from 85% to 95% turns your 3% profit into a 30% loss. Monitoring utilization is mandatory for leveraged strategies.

Supply Rate Derivation

Supply Rate = Borrow Rate × Utilization × (1 − Reserve Factor) If borrow = 4%, utilization = 80%, reserve factor = 15%: Supply rate = 4% × 0.80 × 0.85 = 2.72%

This is why supply rates are always lower than borrow rates — only the utilized portion generates yield, and the protocol takes a cut.

Health Factor & Liquidation

Your health factor tells you how close you are to being liquidated. Below 1.0, liquidators can forcibly close your position at a loss to you.

Health Factor Formula

Health Factor = (Collateral Value × Liquidation Threshold) / Debt Value Example: Collateral: 10 ETH × $3,000 = $30,000 Liquidation threshold: 82.5% Debt: $15,000 USDC HF = ($30,000 × 0.825) / $15,000 = 1.65 ✓ Safe If ETH drops to $2,200: HF = ($22,000 × 0.825) / $15,000 = 1.21 ⚠️ Getting dangerous If ETH drops to $1,820: HF = ($18,200 × 0.825) / $15,000 = 1.001 🔴 About to be liquidated

What Happens During Liquidation

When HF drops below 1.0, liquidator bots buy your collateral at a discount (the liquidation bonus, typically 5-10%). They repay part of your debt and take the discounted collateral as profit. You lose the collateral plus the bonus.

Never Let HF Drop Below 1.5
Professional DeFi operators maintain HF above 1.5 at all times. Set alerts at HF 1.8 (warning) and HF 1.5 (immediate action required). Pre-plan your deleveraging steps before you ever need them.

Leverage Loops

A leverage loop amplifies your yield exposure by recursively supplying and borrowing correlated assets. It's the most popular advanced DeFi strategy — and the most dangerous if mismanaged.

Net Yield = leverage × staking_APY − (leverage − 1) × borrow_APY At 3x leverage, 3.5% staking, 2% borrow cost: Net = 3 × 3.5% − 2 × 2.0% = 10.5% − 4.0% = 6.5% on original capital

Stablecoin Types

Stablecoins are the unit of account in DeFi. They're also one of its biggest risk vectors. Not all stablecoins are created equal — they sit on a spectrum from centralized and reliable to decentralized and fragile.

Three Categories

How they work: Centralized issuer holds reserves (bank deposits, T-bills). Promises to redeem 1:1 for USD.

Advantages: Deepest liquidity. Most widely accepted. Simple mechanism.

Risks: Bank failure (USDC depegged to $0.87 during SVB collapse, March 2023). Regulatory seizure. Censorship — both USDC and USDT can blacklist addresses and freeze your tokens.

How they work: Users lock crypto collateral (ETH, BTC) in smart contracts and mint stablecoins against it. Always over-collateralized (typically 150%+).

DAI: Originally ETH-backed, now >60% backed by USDC and real-world assets. Effectively a wrapped centralized stablecoin.

LUSD (Liquity V1): Purely ETH-backed with immutable contracts. The most decentralized stablecoin in existence — no governance, no admin keys, no upgrades.

How it works: Hold stETH (long), short ETH perpetuals (on centralized exchanges). Net price exposure: zero. Yield comes from staking rewards + positive funding rates.

Reality: USDe is not a stablecoin — it's a structured financial product with a soft peg. During periods of negative funding, the reserve fund is drained.

Risks: Exchange counterparty risk (shorts sit on CEXs). Negative funding. stETH depeg. Liquidity crisis during unwind.

Evaluating Stablecoins

Before holding any stablecoin, answer these five questions. If you can't answer them all, you don't understand the risk you're taking.

The 5-Question Framework

  1. What backs it? — Real USD? Crypto collateral? A derivative position? Nothing?
  2. What happens if the backing fails? — Can you redeem? Is there an insurance fund? Who absorbs the loss?
  3. Can your tokens be frozen? — Does the issuer have a blocklist? (USDC and USDT do.)
  4. Has it survived a stress test? — How did it perform during March 2020, May 2022 (Luna), March 2023 (SVB)?
  5. What's the worst-case scenario? — Complete loss? Temporary depeg? Frozen access?
Dead Category: Algorithmic Stablecoins
Any stablecoin that maintains its peg through its own token's market cap (like UST/LUNA) is fundamentally fragile. The backing is reflexive — when confidence drops, the peg drops, which drops confidence further. UST proved this with $40B evaporating in 72 hours. Avoid this category entirely.

Airdrop Farming

Many protocols reward early users with token airdrops. The strategy: deploy capital into point-earning activities and wait for the token launch.

Framework

  • Expected Value: Estimated FDV × airdrop % × your share of points
  • Cost: Capital × duration × opportunity cost rate + gas
  • Rule: Diversify across 3-5 opportunities. Never borrow to airdrop farm. Treat expected airdrop value as zero until it's realized.
Professional Approach
Calculate your cost basis weekly. If you're "earning" points at a cost of $10 each, and similar protocols airdropped tokens worth $5 per point equivalent, you're underwater. Airdrop farming is speculative — size accordingly.

Smart Contract & Oracle Risk

Every DeFi position sits on a foundation of code and data feeds. When either fails, your funds are at risk — regardless of how "safe" the strategy looks on paper.

Smart Contract Risk

The code might have bugs. Even audited code. Even code that's been live for years.

ExploitAmount LostWhat HappenedWas It Audited?

Mitigation Framework

  • Time is the best audit. Protocols with >$1B TVL for >2 years without incident have passed the most rigorous test possible.
  • Audit quality varies. Trail of Bits, OpenZeppelin, Spearbit are top tier. Unknown auditors are nearly meaningless.
  • Bug bounties signal commitment. A $1M Immunefi bounty means the team is serious. A $10k bounty is cosmetic.
  • Max 20% of capital in any single contract system.

Oracle Risk

DeFi protocols need price data from external sources. Wrong prices → wrong liquidations, exploitable swaps, drained pools.

  • Flash loan manipulation: Borrow millions in one transaction to manipulate a price oracle, exploit a protocol, repay — all in one block.
  • Stale prices: Oracle stops updating during extreme volatility. Liquidations fire at wrong prices.
  • Defense: Prefer protocols using Chainlink (multi-node, battle-tested). Be cautious with custom TWAP oracles.

Liquidity & Depeg Risk

Two risks that feel invisible until they aren't: the inability to exit, and the failure of assets you assumed were stable.

Liquidity Risk

You need to exit but can't — or can only exit at a massive loss.

  • Lending pool at 100% utilization: Your supplied assets are fully borrowed. You cannot withdraw.
  • Thin DEX pool: Your $100k position can only exit with 5% slippage.
  • Bridge congestion: You need to move assets urgently but the bridge is paused.

Mitigation Rules

Position Sizing
Never be more than 5% of a pool's TVL. Check utilization trends before supplying. For positions >$50k, verify exit slippage before entry. Keep capital on L1 Ethereum for maximum optionality during crises.

Cascading Liquidations

DeFi's most dangerous systemic pattern. Liquidation cascades are reflexive — the act of liquidating positions creates the conditions for more liquidations.

The Cascade Sequence

Phase 1 — TRIGGER External shock → price drops → positions approach liquidation threshold Phase 2 — FIRST WAVE Most leveraged positions hit threshold → liquidator bots buy collateral at discount → sell on DEXs for profit Phase 3 — SELL PRESSURE Liquidators dumping collateral → pushes price down further On thin liquidity, impact is massive Phase 4 — SECOND WAVE Additional price drop → next tier of positions liquidated → more collateral sold → more sell pressure Phase 5 — LIQUIDITY EXHAUSTION DEX pools absorb too much selling → slippage becomes extreme → liquidations become unprofitable → liquidators stop bidding Phase 6 — BAD DEBT Positions that can't be profitably liquidated → protocol absorbs loss → insurance fund / safety module stakers take the hit

How to Survive

  • Monitor liquidation walls on DeFiLlama (at what price levels are large positions liquidated?)
  • Keep HF above 1.5 at all times — cascades move fast
  • Pre-plan deleveraging steps and have gas ETH ready on the right chain
  • Remember: gas costs spike during crashes (your $5 deleverage might cost $200)
  • Maintain 20-30% in stablecoins or un-deployed assets as dry powder
The Correlation Trap
In a systemic event, all DeFi correlates to 1. Your "diversified" portfolio across 5 protocols is actually one trade: long DeFi infrastructure. The only real hedge is position sizing and cash reserves.

Practice Exercise

Weekly Drill
Pick one protocol you're deployed in. Map its full liquidity picture: which DEX pools does it depend on? What's the depth? What's the utilization? What would happen if 10% of TVL exited in 24 hours? After doing this for 4 weeks, you'll start seeing liquidity structures instead of just numbers.

Liquidity Red Flags

  • Lending pool utilization consistently above 85%
  • Your position is >5% of a pool's total TVL
  • TVL declining for 3+ consecutive weeks
  • Only one DEX pool for the asset with <$5M liquidity

Skill 3: Liquidation Analysis

Liquidations are DeFi's clearing mechanism. Understanding where liquidation walls sit tells you where the system's stress points are — and where cascades will trigger.

Reading Liquidation Walls

That selling pressure will push price through $2,800 toward $2,600, triggering the $500M wall. This is a cascade scenario.

Position Sizing Rules

  • Maximum 20% of total DeFi capital in any single protocol
  • Maximum 10% in any single strategy
  • Keep 20-30% accessible within 24 hours (dry powder)
  • Total DeFi allocation = a defined % of net worth, not everything

Deployment Checklist

Before deploying capital into any new DeFi position, work through this checklist. If you can't check every item, don't deploy.

Protocol Evaluation

  • Can name the yield source in one sentence (borrowing demand, trading fees, funding rates, etc.)
  • Verified: protocol has been audited by a reputable firm (Trail of Bits, OZ, Spearbit)
  • Verified: protocol has held significant TVL for >6 months without incident
  • Verified: oracle system is Chainlink or equivalent multi-node solution
  • Verified: governance can't unilaterally drain funds (timelock, multisig)

Position Setup

  • Position size is ≤10% of total DeFi capital
  • Can exit at my position size with <1% slippage
  • Health factor is above 1.5 (if leveraged)
  • Exit triggers are defined and written down
  • Worst-case scenario (total loss) is survivable for my portfolio

Ongoing Management

  • Daily: check portfolio dashboard, scan for incidents (5 min)
  • Weekly: calculate net PnL, update position tracker, check governance proposals (30 min)
  • Monthly: full portfolio review vs benchmarks, kill underperformers (60 min)
  • Quarterly: zoom out — am I adding value as an active operator? (2 hours)
Final Mental Model
"Where does the yield come from?" If you can't answer this in one sentence, you don't understand the position. Every yield in DeFi has a source: someone is paying for something. If the source is "new depositors," it might be a Ponzi. If the source is "token emissions," it's subsidized and will compress. Name the source, or don't deploy.
